Question: Your question here. What is the relationship between the age at first conception for female lemurs and the number of known offspring they produce throughout their lifetime at the Duke Lemur Center?

Introduction: Your introduction here. The Duke Lemur Center dataset is a comprehensive record of the lemurs that have been housed at the center from 1966 to 2014. This dataset is invaluable for conservation efforts, providing extensive details on individual lemurs, including metrics on reproduction, lifespan, and health. For this project, we are particularly interested in understanding the reproductive success of female lemurs. The columns of interest for our question are “dam_age_at_concep_y” (the female lemur’s age at conception in years) and “n_known_offspring” (the number of known offspring a lemur has produced). By analyzing these variables, we aim to investigate if there is any correlation between the age at which female lemurs first conceive and their overall reproductive output.

Approach: Your approach here. To explore the relationship between the age at first conception and the number of offspring, we will perform a linear regression analysis using the lm() function. This will allow us to assess whether earlier or later conception age impacts the total number of offspring produced. We will also use ggplot2 to create two types of visualizations: a scatter plot with a regression line to depict the relationship, and a bar plot to show the average number of offspring at different conception age categories. These plots will help us visualize any trends and examine the distribution of reproductive success across different ages.

Analysis:

# Your R code here
# Load necessary libraries
library(tidyverse)
library(ggplot2)
library(broom)

# Filter out unnecessary data and handle NA values
lemur_data <- lemurs %>%
  filter(!is.na(dam_age_at_concep_y) & !is.na(n_known_offspring))

# Linear regression of offspring count on dam's age at first conception
lm_fit <- lm(n_known_offspring ~ dam_age_at_concep_y, data = lemur_data)
lm_summary <- summary(lm_fit)

# Scatter plot with regression line
scatter_plot <- ggplot(lemur_data, aes(x = dam_age_at_concep_y, y = n_known_offspring)) +
  geom_point(alpha = 0.6) +
  geom_smooth(method = "lm", se = FALSE, color = "blue") +
  labs(title = "Scatter Plot of Known Offspring vs. Female Lemur's Age at First Conception", x = "Female Lemur's Age at First Conception (years)", y = "Number of Known Offspring") +
  theme_minimal()

# Bar plot of average offspring by age category
age_category_offspring <- lemur_data %>%
  mutate(age_category = cut_width(dam_age_at_concep_y, width = 2, boundary = 0)) %>%
  group_by(age_category) %>%
  summarise(Average_Offspring = mean(n_known_offspring), .groups = 'drop')

bar_plot <- ggplot(age_category_offspring, aes(x = age_category, y = Average_Offspring, fill = age_category)) +
  geom_bar(stat = "identity") +
  labs(title = "Average Number of Known Offspring by Female Lemur's Age Category at First Conception", x = "Female Lemur's Age Category at First Conception", y = "Average Number of Known Offspring") +
  theme_minimal() +
  theme(legend.position = "none", plot.title = element_text(size = rel(1.0)))

# Output plots and regression summary
list(Regression_Summary = lm_summary,
     Scatter_Plot = scatter_plot,
     Bar_Plot = bar_plot)

Discussion: Your discussion of results here. The data presented in the scatter and bar plots suggest a complex relationship between the age of a dam at first conception and her subsequent reproductive output. The scatter plot indicates a positive but subtle correlation, with older dams at first conception tending to have slightly more offspring. This trend, however, is not definitive due to considerable variability, suggesting that other factors may also play a significant role in reproductive success. In the bar plot, the average number of known offspring is variable across age categories, with notable peaks in the youngest [4,6] and oldest [10,12] and [20,22] brackets. While this could indicate higher fertility rates or survivorship of offspring for dams conceiving at these ages, the influence of outliers or a smaller sample size in these extreme categories cannot be discounted. The lower averages observed in mid-range age categories might reflect a more stable but less prolific reproductive phase. The data implies that while age at first conception has an impact on the number of known offspring, the relationship is not strictly linear and is potentially moderated by a myriad of biological, environmental, and management factors that warrant further investigation.
