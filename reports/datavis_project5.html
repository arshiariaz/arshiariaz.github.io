Question: Your question here. How does the duration on the best sellers list (total weeks) correlate with the debut rank of books, and does this trend differ based on the initial popularity (debut rank)?

Introduction: Your introduction here. The nyt_titles.csv dataset provides a rich historical record of literary success, featuring an array of books that have been on the New York Times Best Sellers list. This dataset not only captures the titles and authors of these books but also chronicles their performance over time, including the year of listing, the number of weeks each book remained a best seller, and the book’s rank upon debut. This analysis seeks to delve into the relationship between a book’s initial impact (debut rank) and its endurance on the list (total weeks), with an intention towards uncovering the underlying patterns that might influence a book’s popularity and longevity in the public eye.

Approach: Your approach here. To unravel the complexities within the dataset, this analysis will utilize of k-means clustering, focusing on the ‘total_weeks’ a book stays on the best sellers list and its ‘debut_rank’. The elbow method, a decisive technique for identifying the point where the increase in the number of clusters no longer leads to a substantial decrease in within-cluster variation, will guide the selection of the optimal number of clusters for the k-means algorithm. Following this, a scatter plot will be constructed to visualize the clusters. This plot will not only display the distribution of books within the determined clusters but will also feature the calculated centroids of each cluster. These centroids serve as a navigational tool, steering our interpretation of each cluster’s core characteristics and facilitating a clear delineation between the distinct groupings identified in the analysis.

Analysis:

# Your R code here
# Selecting relevant columns for clustering
clustering_data <- nyt_data %>% select(total_weeks, debut_rank)

# Elbow Method to find the optimal number of clusters
wcss <- map_dbl(1:10, ~kmeans(clustering_data, .x, nstart = 10)$tot.withinss)

# Create a plot for the Elbow Method
elbow_plot <- tibble(clusters = 1:10, wcss = wcss) %>%
  ggplot(aes(clusters, wcss)) +
  geom_line() +
  geom_point() +
  geom_vline(xintercept = 3, linetype = "dashed", color = "red") +  # Assuming 3 is the optimal number of clusters
  labs(title = "Elbow Method For Optimal Clusters", x = "Number of clusters", y = "Within cluster sum of squares") +
  theme_minimal()

# Print the elbow plot
print(elbow_plot)

# Perform k-means clustering with the determined optimal number of clusters
optimal_clusters <- 3  # This value should be determined from the elbow plot
km_fit <- kmeans(clustering_data, centers = optimal_clusters, nstart = 10)
clustering_data$cluster <- as.factor(km_fit$cluster)

# Plotting the clusters
kmeans_plot <- ggplot(clustering_data, aes(x = total_weeks, y = debut_rank, color = cluster)) +
  geom_point(size = 2) +  # Adjust the size if needed
  geom_point(data = as.data.frame(km_fit$centers), aes(x = total_weeks, y = debut_rank), 
             shape = 8, size = 4, color = "black") +  # Centroids
  scale_color_brewer(palette = "Set1") +  # A refined color palette
  labs(title = "K-means Clustering of Books",
       x = "Total Weeks on Best Sellers List",
       y = "Debut Rank",
       color = "Cluster") +
  theme_minimal()

# Print the k-means plot
print(kmeans_plot)

Discussion: Your discussion of results here. The elbow method plot serves as a foundational step in our k-means clustering analysis, providing a statistically sound method for determining the optimal number of clusters. Upon examining the plot, we observe a distinct ‘elbow’ at the three-cluster mark, indicating that increasing the number of clusters beyond three leads to a less significant decrease in within-cluster variance. This suggests that three is the most suitable number of clusters for our dataset, allowing us to strike a balance between detailed segmentation and manageable cluster interpretation.

Following this quantitative guidance, the k-means clustering was performed, revealing three distinct groupings of books based on their longevity on the best sellers list and initial popularity. Cluster 1, characterized by books with fewer total weeks on the list and a broad range of debut ranks, could represent titles that quickly capture public interest but fail to maintain it over time, which might be due to niche appeal or transient trends. Cluster 2 encompasses titles with moderate staying power and higher debut ranks, likely reflecting books that generated immediate attention, possibly through strong marketing campaigns or author recognition, and maintained a presence on the list for an average duration. Lastly, Cluster 3 consists of books with both high debut ranks and longer tenure on the list, suggesting a combination of strong initial interest and sustained popularity, which could be attributed to widespread appeal, critical acclaim, or ongoing marketing support.

These trends provide insight into the dynamics of book popularity. Books in Cluster 3, for example, might benefit from word-of-mouth recommendations, suggesting that their quality resonates with readers, leading to sustained sales. In contrast, Cluster 1’s books might rely more on initial buzz or specific circumstances for their early success, which does not translate into long-term popularity. Understanding these patterns could inform publishers and authors about the factors that contribute to a book’s success on the best sellers list.
